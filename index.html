ASQA assessment requirements

Evidence must include: preparation of materials, safe use of tools, application of 3+ propagation techniques (seed, cutting, division), records of environmental monitoring, and completion of workplace documentation.

Biosecurity hygiene

Clean tools with 70% ethanol or bleach between batches; quarantine incoming plant material; prevent spread of Phytophthora and myrtle rust.

🌱 Selecting propagation material

Seed: Use fresh, viable seed from reliable supplier or collected under permit. Record provenance. Cuttings: Select healthy, disease-free stock plants; take semi-hardwood or softwood at correct phenological stage. Grafting: Ensure compatible rootstock and scion (same genus/family). Division: Choose vigorous clumping plants (grasses, ferns, lilies).

🪴 Core propagation techniques (Seed, Cutting, Grafting, Division)

Seed propagation

  • Pre-treatment: scarification (hot water, sandpaper) for hard-coated seeds; stratification (cold) for temperate species.
  • Sowing depth = 2–3× seed diameter.
  • Use sterile seed-raising mix; keep moist but not waterlogged.
  • Cover with vermiculite to reduce damping-off.

Cutting propagation

  • Softwood cuttings (spring): 5–10 cm, remove lower leaves, dip in rooting hormone (IBA 2000–3000ppm).
  • Semi-hardwood (summer): use heel cutting, wound base.
  • Hardwood cuttings (dormant): 15–25 cm, insert into sandy medium.
  • Maintain high humidity (mist/fog) and bottom heat (22–25°C).

Grafting & budding

  • Whip & tongue, cleft graft, T-budding.
  • Match cambium layers, seal with grafting tape/wax.
  • Keep graft union humid until callus forms.
  • Remove rootstock suckers promptly.

Division & layering

  • Division: separate rhizomes, crowns or clumps (ornamental grasses, daylilies). Ensure each division has roots and shoots.
  • Layering: peg down low branch, cover with soil, sever when rooted.
  • Ideal for shrubs (Magnolia, Rhododendron).

💧 Post-propagation care & hardening off

🌿 Monitoring for plant health

Record daily observations: root development (visible at drainage holes), new leaf growth, signs of disease (damping-off, grey mould), pest presence (fungus gnats, aphids). Adjust misting frequency as roots form; reduce humidity gradually. Hardening off: Over 7–14 days, increase light exposure and reduce water to acclimatise plants to external conditions before transplanting.

Nutrient management

Apply dilute liquid fertiliser (N-P-K 20:10:20) at ¼ strength once true leaves appear or cuttings show roots. Avoid high nitrogen before hardening.

Integrated pest management

Preventative: good hygiene, air movement. Use beneficial insects (lacewings) for aphids; apply neem oil for mites; remove infected material.

Industry glossary & resources

Callus: Undifferentiated tissue that forms at cutting base prior to rooting.

Stratification: Cold treatment to break seed dormancy.

Scarification: Mechanical or thermal treatment to allow water uptake.

IBA: Indole-3-butyric acid – rooting hormone.

Damping-off: Fungal disease killing seedlings at soil line.

Hardening off: Acclimatisation process.

Provenance: Geographic origin of propagation material.
