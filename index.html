Context: Propagation in Conservation & Ecosystem Management

Why Propagation Matters in CEM

In Conservation and Ecosystem Management, propagation skills are essential for:

  • Ecological Restoration: Producing local-provenance plants for revegetation, riparian restoration, and habitat reconstruction
  • Threatened Species Recovery: Ex-situ conservation through seed banking and propagation of rare and endangered Tasmanian flora
  • Weed Management: Propagating competitive native species for suppression of environmental weeds
  • Community Engagement: Running propagation workshops and community nurseries for Landcare and Bushcare groups
  • Research & Monitoring: Supplying plants for ecological research, climate adaptation trials, and genetic studies
Industry Relevance & Employment Pathways

Graduates with propagation expertise are employed in:

  • Nurseries: Native plant nurseries (e.g., Understorey Network, local councils)
  • Government Agencies: NRE Tas, Parks & Wildlife, local government revegetation teams
  • Consultancies: Environmental consulting firms delivering offset and restoration projects
  • NRM Organisations: Landcare, NRM South, Cradle Coast NRM
  • Botanic Gardens: Royal Tasmanian Botanical Gardens conservation programs

Tasmanian Context: Unique Flora & Conservation Priorities

Tasmania is a global biodiversity hotspot with over 2,300 native vascular plant species, of which approximately 580 (25%) are endemic. Propagation skills are critical for:

  • Recovery of threatened species like Epacris stuartii (Critically Endangered), Athrotaxis cupressoides (Vulnerable), and Prasophyllum apoxychilum (Critically Endangered)
  • Restoration of threatened ecological communities including Tasmanian rainforest, native grasslands, and coastal heathlands
  • Climate change adaptation – propagating species from warmer, drier provenances to future-proof plantings
  • Dieback management (Phytophthora cinnamomi) – producing resistant stock for affected areas

1. Seed Propagation

Tasmanian natives: Eucalyptus globulus, Acacia melanoxylon, Banksia marginata, Nothofagus cunninghamii

Pre-treatments:
• Hot water scarification: Acacia, Kennedia (pour boiling water, soak 12-24h)
• Cold stratification: Pomaderris, Eucalyptus coccifera (2-5°C, 6-10 weeks)
• Smoke water: Banksia, Stylidium, many heath species
• Sowing depth: 2-3× seed diameter; fine seeds surface sow

Media: Sterile seed-raising mix, low phosphorus for natives.

2. Vegetative Cuttings

Softwood cuttings (spring-early summer): Leptospermum, Melaleuca, Correa – IBA 2000-3000ppm, high humidity.
Semi-hardwood (late summer-autumn): Prostanthera, Westringia – IBA 3000ppm, mist.
Hardwood (winter): deciduous species, some eucalypts from juvenile stock.
Rooting hormone (IBA): hastens rooting, increases percentage, improves root quality and uniformity.

3. Division & Layering

Division: Ideal for clumping species: Poa labillardierei, Lomandra longifolia, Gahnia grandis, Dianella tasmanica, Dicksonia antarctica. Divide in early spring, ensure each division has 3-5 healthy shoots and roots.
Layering: Peg low branch into soil, cover, sever when rooted. Works for Rhododendron, Prostanthera.

4. Grafting & Budding

Used for cultivars and difficult-to-root species. Whip & tongue for equal diameter scion/rootstock; cleft graft for larger rootstock.
Key principles: Match cambium layers, seal with grafting tape/wax, keep humid until callus forms.
Tasmanian context: Grafting of Eucalyptus cultivars, Telopea truncata for improved flowering.

Work Planning, Parent Plants & Ethical Sourcing

🌿 Parent Plant Selection

Definition: The plant from which propagation material (cuttings, seed, divisions) is collected.
Required qualities:
• Young, vigorous growth habit
• Healthy, pest and disease free
• True to type (genetically true to species/cultivar)
• Not governed by Plant Breeder's Rights (PBR)
• Well-watered the day before collection
• Avoid plants in flower or fruiting (for cuttings)

🧼 Hygiene Protocols (5 key practices)

1. Sterilise equipment: 70% ethanol or 1% bleach solution on benches, tools, pots.
2. Clean floors: Daily sweeping and disinfecting.
3. Footbaths: At propagation area entrance to prevent pathogen spread.
4. Personal hygiene: Wear disposable gloves, wash hands, clean overalls.
5. Quarantine: Isolate new plant material for 2 weeks, separate propagation zones.
Additional: Use sterile propagation media, avoid bringing soil into greenhouse.

Ethical Collection Guidelines & Permits (Tasmania)

Legislation: Under Nature Conservation Act 2002 and Threatened Species Protection Act 1995, collection of native plant material from public land or for threatened species requires a scientific permit from NRE Tas.
The 20% Rule: Collect no more than 20% of seed/cuttings from any population to ensure natural regeneration and maintain genetic diversity.
Permit contacts: FloraPermit.Enquiries@nre.tas.gov.au
Aboriginal heritage: Due diligence required before ground-disturbing works.

📦 Storage of Collected Material

Immediate post-collection:
• Place in sealed polythene bags with damp paper towel
• Keep in cool, shaded area
• Transport in insulated esky with ice bricks
• Process within 4-6 hours for optimal viability
Refrigeration: Store at 2-5°C for up to 48 hours if processing delayed.

Propagation Techniques, Equipment & Maintenance

🧪 Rooting Hormone (IBA)

Why use rooting hormone?
1. Hastens root initiation
2. Increases percentage of cuttings that form roots
3. Increases number and quality of roots per cutting
4. Improves uniformity of rooting
Application: Dip base of cutting (1-2cm) into IBA powder/gel immediately before inserting into media.

🔥 Heat Bed & Misting Systems

Reasons for bottom heat:
1. Encourages active cell division at cutting base
2. Promotes faster root initiation (optimal 22-25°C)
Reasons for misting:
1. Keeps tops cool and reduces transpiration
2. Prevents wilting while roots develop
3. Maintains high humidity (85-95%) around foliage

🌱 Seed Germination Factors

Essential factors:
• Moisture: Consistent but not waterlogged
• Temperature: Optimal varies by species (15-25°C for most natives)
• Oxygen: Well-aerated media prevents damping off
• Light: Some species require light for germination (surface sow), others darkness (cover with vermiculite)

🧫 Containers for Cuttings

Common container types:
• Tubes: 50mm, 75mm, 100mm depths for deep-rooted species
• Cell trays: 40-cell, 50-cell, 84-cell for high-density propagation
• Plug trays: For automated filling and transplanting
• Root trainers: Air-pruning design for eucalypts and acacias

Aftercare, Nutrition & Environmental Monitoring

💧 Nutrient Application Stages

Before roots develop (seeds not germinated / cuttings not rooted):
• No fertiliser required
• Seeds contain endosperm for initial growth
• Cuttings have no root system to uptake nutrients
After germination / root development:
• Introduce dilute liquid fertiliser (¼ strength)
• Use low-phosphorus formula for native plants (Proteaceae sensitive)
• Apply weekly or fortnightly during active growth

🐛 Common Problems in Greenhouse

Five common problems:
1. Seeds won't germinate – check dormancy requirements, temperature, moisture
2. Cuttings wilt or die – inadequate humidity, poor parent material, pathogens
3. Cuttings won't root – insufficient hormone, wrong temperature, poor timing
4. Over or under watering – monitor media moisture, adjust misting
5. Pests and diseases – damping-off (fungal), Botrytis, root rots, aphids, fungus gnats

🗑️ Nursery Waste Disposal

Broken pots, trays, tubes: Landfill or recycling (where facilities exist)
Diseased plant material: Landfill, or solarisation in sealed black plastic bags (6 weeks in summer)
Used growing media: Compost or recycle to garden (if disease-free)
General green waste: Compost (hot composting to kill pathogens)
Weeds with seeds, bulbs, rhizomes: Landfill, or weed tea (ferment in water), or solarisation

Glossary & Essential Resources

Comprehensive Glossary of Propagation Terms

Asexual propagation
Propagation without seeds using vegetative parts (cuttings, division, grafting) producing genetically identical offspring.
Bottom heat
Heating cables or mats placed under propagation media to warm root zone and stimulate rooting.
Callus
Undifferentiated plant tissue that forms at wound sites, precursor to root or shoot development.
Cambium
Thin layer of actively dividing cells between xylem and phloem; must be aligned in grafting.
Damping-off
Fungal disease (Pythium, Rhizoctonia) killing seedlings at soil line, favoured by wet conditions.
Dormancy (seed)
State where viable seed fails to germinate even under favourable conditions; requires breaking treatment.
Endemic
Species native to and restricted to a particular geographic region (Tasmania has ~580 endemic plants).
Ex-situ conservation
Conservation of species outside natural habitat (seed banks, botanic gardens, nurseries).
Hardening off
Gradual acclimatisation of greenhouse-grown plants to outdoor conditions.
IBA (Indole-3-butyric acid)
Synthetic rooting hormone that stimulates adventitious root formation.
In-situ conservation
Conservation within natural habitat; protecting wild populations.
Lignotuber
Woody swelling at base containing dormant buds; enables regeneration after fire (eucalypts, banksias).
Local provenance
Plant material collected from local geographic area, adapted to local conditions.
Misting system
Automatic irrigation system producing fine water droplets to maintain high humidity.
Orthodox seed
Seed that can be dried to low moisture (5-8%) and stored long-term at low temperatures.
Parent plant
Source plant from which propagation material (cuttings, seed, divisions) is collected.
Phytophthora cinnamomi
Soil-borne pathogen causing root rot and dieback in many Australian natives.
Plant Breeder's Rights (PBR)
Intellectual property protection for new plant varieties; propagation requires licence.
Recalcitrant seed
Seed that cannot be dried or stored; must be sown fresh (e.g., Nothofagus, rainforest species).
Rooting hormone
Compound (natural or synthetic) applied to cuttings to stimulate root development.
Scarification
Mechanical or thermal treatment to break physical seed coat dormancy.
Scion
Upper portion of graft that provides desired shoot characteristics.
Smoke water
Aqueous solution of smoke compounds used to break seed dormancy in fire-adapted species.
Stratification
Cold, moist treatment to break physiological seed dormancy (2-5°C for weeks).
Strike
Term for successful rooting of a cutting or germination of seed.
Understock/Rootstock
Lower portion of graft providing root system.
Vermiculite
Hydrated mineral used in propagation media for water retention and aeration.
Whip and tongue graft
Grafting technique for equal-diameter scion and rootstock with interlocking cuts.
Xylem
Plant tissue conducting water and minerals; outer layers involved in grafting union.
