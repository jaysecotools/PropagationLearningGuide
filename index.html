Context: Propagation in Conservation & Ecosystem Management

Why Propagation Matters in CEM

In Conservation and Ecosystem Management, propagation skills are essential for:

  • Ecological Restoration: Producing local-provenance plants for revegetation, riparian restoration, and habitat reconstruction
  • Threatened Species Recovery: Ex-situ conservation through seed banking and propagation of rare and endangered Tasmanian flora (some requiring specialised techniques, e.g. orchid mycorrhiza) (e.g., Epacris stuartii, Athrotaxis cupressoides)
  • Weed Management: Propagating competitive native species to support weed suppression as part of integrated weed management (e.g. gorse, blackberry, boneseed)
  • Community Engagement: Running propagation workshops and community nurseries for Landcare and Bushcare groups
  • Research & Monitoring: Supplying plants for ecological research, climate adaptation trials, and genetic studies
  • Carbon Farming: Producing stock for biodiverse carbon and restoration plantings
Industry Relevance & Employment Pathways

Graduates with propagation expertise are employed in:

  • Nurseries: Native plant nurseries (e.g., Understorey Network, local councils, private native nurseries)
  • Government Agencies: NRE Tas, Parks & Wildlife, local government revegetation teams
  • Consultancies: Environmental consulting firms delivering offset and restoration projects
  • NRM Organisations: Landcare, NRM South, Cradle Coast NRM, Wildcare
  • Botanic Gardens: Royal Tasmanian Botanical Gardens conservation programs
  • Mining & Infrastructure: Mine site rehabilitation and infrastructure offset planting
Growing Sector
Ecological restoration is a growing sector in Tasmania, supported by significant government, NRM and private investment, with increasing demand for skilled practitioners
Local Provenance
Plants sourced locally often show improved survival in restoration projects, with some studies suggesting around 50% higher performance on average, though results vary by species and site
Threatened Species
Many endemic Tasmanian species benefit from ex-situ conservation efforts such as seed banking and propagation programs, supporting restoration and safeguarding biodiversity

Tasmanian Context: Unique Flora & Conservation Priorities

Tasmania is a global biodiversity hotspot with over 2,300 native vascular plant species, with approximately 550–600 endemic. Propagation skills are critical for:

  • Recovery of threatened species like Epacris stuartii (Critically Endangered), Athrotaxis cupressoides (Vulnerable), Prasophyllum apoxychilum (Critically Endangered), and Banksia serratuloides (Endangered)
  • Restoration of threatened ecological communities including Tasmanian rainforest (e.g., myrtle-beech forest), native grasslands (Themeda triandra dominant), and coastal heathlands
  • Climate change adaptation – propagating species from warmer, drier provenances to future-proof plantings (assisted migration within natural range)
  • Dieback management (Phytophthora cinnamomi) – producing resistant stock for affected areas and implementing hygiene protocols
  • Post-fire restoration: Following catastrophic fires, propagation of fire-sensitive species like King Billy Pine becomes critical for recovery
2019-2020 bushfires affected over 200,000 ha of Tasmanian native vegetation
Wetland restoration requires specialised propagation of aquatic and riparian species

1. Seed Propagation

Tasmanian natives: Eucalyptus globulus, Acacia melanoxylon, Banksia marginata, Nothofagus cunninghamii, Melaleuca ericifolia, Allocasuarina verticillata

Pre-treatments:
• Hot water scarification: Acacia, Kennedia, Indigofera, Hardenbergia (pour boiling water, soak 12-24h)
• Cold stratification: Pomaderris, Eucalyptus coccifera, Richea, Astelia (2-5°C, 6-10 weeks in moist sand/vermiculite)
• Smoke water: Banksia, Stylidium, Anigozanthos, many heath species (Epacridaceae) – apply as pre-soak or mist
• Mechanical scarification: For hard-seeded species – nicking seed coat with file
• Sowing depth: 2-3× seed diameter; fine seeds surface sow with thin vermiculite cover
Media: Sterile seed-raising mix, low phosphorus for natives (Proteaceae sensitive). pH 5.5-6.5 ideal.

Seed longevity: Orthodox seeds (Eucalyptus, Acacia) can be stored at 5°C for 5-10 years. Recalcitrant seeds (Nothofagus, Atherosperma) must be sown within weeks.

2. Vegetative Cuttings

Softwood cuttings (spring-early summer): Leptospermum, Melaleuca, Correa, Callistemon, Westringia – IBA 2000-3000ppm, high humidity (85-95%), bottom heat 22-25°C.
Semi-hardwood (late summer-autumn): Prostanthera, Westringia, Hakea, Grevillea – IBA 3000-4000ppm, mist system, partial shade.
Hardwood (winter): deciduous species, some eucalypts from juvenile stock, Populus, Salix (for riparian restoration) – IBA 1000-2000ppm, outdoor beds.
Root cuttings: For species like Banksia serrata, Acacia verticillata – collect in winter, 5-10cm sections, horizontal placement.
Rooting hormone (IBA): hastens root initiation, increases percentage, improves root quality and uniformity. Available in powder, gel, and liquid formulations.

3. Division & Layering

Division: Ideal for clumping species: Poa labillardierei, Lomandra longifolia, Gahnia grandis, Dianella tasmanica, Dicksonia antarctica, Stipa stipoides, Carex appressa. Divide in early spring or autumn, ensure each division has 3-5 healthy shoots and fibrous roots. Use sharp spade or knife.
Layering: Peg low branch into soil, cover, sever when rooted. Works for Rhododendron, Prostanthera, Boronia, Correa. Air layering for taller species – girdle branch, apply rooting hormone, wrap with moist sphagnum and plastic.

Fern propagation: Spore collection for Dicksonia, Blechnum, Polystichum – requires sterile media and high humidity.

4. Grafting & Budding

Used for cultivars and difficult-to-root species. Whip & tongue for equal diameter scion/rootstock; cleft graft for larger rootstock; side-veneer graft for evergreen species.
Key principles: Match cambium layers (essential for vascular connection), seal with grafting tape/wax, keep humid until callus forms (2-6 weeks).
Tasmanian context: Grafting of Eucalyptus cultivars, Telopea truncata for improved flowering, Prostanthera for colour forms. Rootstocks: Eucalyptus gunnii for cold tolerance, Eucalyptus globulus for vigour.

T-budding: Summer technique for Rosa (native roses) and selected fruit trees used in conservation plantings.

Work Planning, Parent Plants & Ethical Sourcing

🌿 Parent Plant Selection

Definition: The plant from which propagation material (cuttings, seed, divisions) is collected.
Required qualities:
• Young, vigorous growth habit (1-3 year old wood optimal for cuttings)
• Healthy, pest and disease free – inspect for aphids, scale, fungal lesions
• True to type (genetically true to species/cultivar) – verify identification
• Not governed by Plant Breeder’s Rights (PBR) unless licensed
• Well-watered the day before collection (turgid tissue improves rooting)
• Avoid plants in flower or fruiting (for cuttings – diverts energy)
• Collect from mid-morning after dew has evaporated to reduce disease risk

🧼 Hygiene Protocols (10 key practices)

1. Sterilise equipment: 70% ethanol or bleach solution (typically 0.5–1% available chlorine) on benches, tools, pots between batches.
2. Clean floors: Daily sweeping and disinfecting with quaternary ammonium compounds.
3. Footbaths: At propagation area entrance with Virkon or bleach solution, changed daily.
4. Personal hygiene: Wear disposable gloves, wash hands, clean overalls, hairnets for seed handling.
5. Quarantine: Isolate new plant material for 2 weeks, separate propagation zones.
6. Sterile propagation media: Pasteurised or commercially sterilised to eliminate pathogens.
7. Avoid cross-contamination: Separate benches for seeds, cuttings, and established plants.
8. Water quality: Use clean, potable water; avoid recycled water for misting.
9. Air circulation: Maintain airflow to reduce humidity and fungal spore accumulation.
10. Record keeping: Track disease outbreaks and hygiene interventions.

Ethical Collection Guidelines & Permits (Tasmania)

Legislation: Under Nature Conservation Act 2002 and Threatened Species Protection Act 1995, collection of native plant material from public land or for threatened species requires a scientific permit from NRE Tas. Private land requires landholder permission.
The 20% Rule: Collect no more than 20% of seed/cuttings from any population to ensure natural regeneration and maintain genetic diversity. For rare species, collect less than 5% or only fallen seed.
Permit contacts: FloraPermit.Enquiries@nre.tas.gov.au | Ph: 1300 368 550
Aboriginal heritage: Due diligence required before ground-disturbing works. Consult with Aboriginal Heritage Tasmania.
Biosecurity: Check for restrictions on moving plant material between regions to prevent spread of Phytophthora, myrtle rust, or other pathogens.

📦 Storage of Collected Material

Immediate post-collection:
• Place in sealed polythene bags with damp paper towel (not soaking)
• Keep in cool, shaded area away from direct sunlight
• Transport in insulated esky with ice bricks (2-5°C)
• Process as soon as possible (ideally within the same day); up to 24 hours if refrigerated
Refrigeration: Store at 2-5°C for up to 48 hours if processing delayed. Do not freeze vegetative material.
Seed storage: Dry seeds to 5-8% moisture, store in airtight containers at 5°C for orthodox species. Recalcitrant seeds store moist (not wet) at 2-5°C for short periods only.

Propagation Techniques, Equipment & Maintenance

🧪 Rooting Hormone (IBA) - Detailed

Why use rooting hormone?
1. Hastens root initiation by 7-14 days
2. Can significantly increase the percentage of cuttings that form roots
3. Increases number and quality of roots per cutting (more fibrous root systems)
4. Improves uniformity of rooting across batches
5. Allows use of less favourable cutting wood
Application: Dip base of cutting (1-2cm) into IBA powder/gel immediately after wounding, tap off excess, insert into media within seconds. Liquid formulations may require pre-soak (5-10 seconds).
Safety: Wear gloves, avoid inhalation, wash hands after use.

🔥 Heat Bed & Misting Systems

Reasons for bottom heat:
1. Encourages active cell division at cutting base (optimal 22-25°C)
2. Promotes faster root initiation (roots form 1-2 weeks earlier)
3. Allows propagation outside ideal seasonal windows
4. Increases rooting percentage by 15-25%
Reasons for misting:
1. Keeps tops cool (reduces leaf temperature by 5-10°C)
2. Reduces transpiration and water stress while roots develop
3. Maintains high humidity (85-95%) around foliage
4. Allows propagation of softwood cuttings in summer
System types: Overhead mist (intermittent), fogging (continuous fine mist), capillary matting for base moisture.

🌱 Seed Germination Factors

Essential factors:
• Moisture: Consistent but not waterlogged – use capillary matting or fine mist
• Temperature: Optimal varies by species (15-25°C for most natives). Thermogradient plates for diverse species.
• Oxygen: Well-aerated media prevents damping off – avoid compaction, use perlite/vermiculite
• Light: Some species require light for germination (surface sow) e.g., Leptospermum, Eucalyptus; others require darkness (cover with 2-3mm vermiculite) e.g., Acacia, Kennedia.
• Dormancy-breaking: Identify and apply appropriate pre-treatment (scarification, stratification, smoke).

🧫 Containers for Cuttings & Seedlings

Common container types:
• Tubes: 50mm, 75mm, 100mm depths – deep tubes for tap-rooted species (Eucalyptus, Allocasuarina)
• Cell trays: 40-cell, 50-cell, 84-cell – high density for seed germination and small cuttings
• Plug trays: 128-200 cell for automated filling and transplanting
• Root trainers: Air-pruning design for eucalypts and acacias – prevents root circling
• Forestry tubes: 150mm depth for large-scale restoration stock
Material: Recyclable polypropylene, biodegradable (paper, coir) for direct planting

📐 Media Mixes by Application
  • Seed raising: 70% fine peat/coir + 30% perlite (sieved to <5mm)
  • Cutting propagation: 50% coarse perlite + 50% peat/coir (or 50:50 sand:peat for hardwood)
  • Tube stock (general): 60% pine bark (5-10mm) + 20% coir + 20% coarse sand + controlled release fertiliser (low phosphorus)
  • Proteaceae mix: 80% coarse sand + 20% peat, no added phosphorus, pH 5.0-5.5

Aftercare, Nutrition & Environmental Monitoring

💧 Nutrient Application Stages

Before roots develop (seeds not germinated / cuttings not rooted):
• No fertiliser required – can inhibit rooting and cause osmotic stress
• Seeds contain endosperm or cotyledons for initial growth (up to 2-4 weeks)
• Cuttings have no root system to uptake nutrients; rely on stored reserves
After germination / root development (2-4 weeks):
• Introduce dilute liquid fertiliser (¼ to ½ strength) – native-specific formulation (low P, high N)
• Use weekly or fortnightly during active growth
• Controlled release fertiliser (CRF) incorporated at potting (3-6 month formulation)
Nutrient deficiencies: Yellowing (N deficiency), purple/red (P deficiency), interveinal chlorosis (Fe/Mg).

🐛 Common Problems in Greenhouse

Five common problems:
1. Seeds won't germinate – check dormancy requirements, temperature, moisture, seed viability
2. Cuttings wilt or die – inadequate humidity, poor parent material, pathogens (Botrytis, Fusarium)
3. Cuttings won't root – insufficient hormone, wrong temperature (too cold or too hot), poor timing
4. Over or under watering – monitor media moisture (should be moist but not saturated). Saturated media causes root rot.
5. Pests and diseases – damping-off (Pythium, Rhizoctonia), Botrytis grey mould, root rots, aphids, thrips, fungus gnats, two-spotted mite.
Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Biological controls (predatory mites, Bacillus thuringiensis), sticky traps, sanitation, approved chemicals as last resort.

🗑️ Nursery Waste Disposal

Broken pots, trays, tubes: Landfill or recycling (where facilities exist – check local council)
Diseased plant material: Landfill, or solarisation in sealed black plastic bags (6 weeks in summer in full sun) to kill pathogens before composting
Used growing media: Compost or recycle to garden (if disease-free) – can be used as soil amendment
General green waste: Compost (hot composting to 60°C+ to kill pathogens and weed seeds)
Weeds with seeds, bulbs, rhizomes: Landfill, or weed tea (ferment in water for 4-6 weeks), or solarisation – never add to standard compost
Chemical containers: Triple rinse, dispose per label instructions, return to ChemClear program where available

📋 Nursery Records

Essential records for ASQA compliance and best practice:
1. Propagation records: Species, date, method, source location, quantity, batch number, expected yield
2. Potting records: Media type, container size, fertiliser applied (type, rate, date), staff initials
3. Daily temperature records: Max/min temperatures, heating system operation, humidity
4. Spray records: Pesticide/fungicide applications, rates, dates, target pest, withholding periods
5. Fertiliser records: Type, concentration (EC), application dates, method (foliar, irrigation)
6. Irrigation records: Frequency, duration, volume, water quality (pH, EC)
7. Sales/dispatch records: Customer, species, quantity, provenance, date
Digital tools: Nursery management software, spreadsheets, or paper logs kept for minimum 5 years.

🌱 Hardening Off Schedule

Gradual acclimatisation to outdoor conditions is critical for survival after sale or planting.

  • Week 1: Reduce misting frequency, increase ventilation, maintain shade (70%)
  • Week 2: Move to 50% shade, increase air movement, reduce watering frequency
  • Week 3: Move to 30% shade, expose to morning sun, reduce fertiliser
  • Week 4: Full outdoor conditions for hardy species, continue monitoring for stress
  • Signs of insufficient hardening: Leaf burn, wilting, stunted growth after planting
