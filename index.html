Provenance & Seed Transfer Zones

For Tasmanian restoration, source seed within the same bioregion (e.g., Ben Lomond, Northern Slopes, West Coast). Climate-adjusted provenancing (collect from lower elevation/warmer sites) helps future-proof plantings.

Fire-adapted species

Many Banksia, Hakea, and some eucalypts are serotinous – collect cones after fire or use heat to extract seed. Smoke water (or smoke disks) enhances germination for many fire-responsive Tasmanian shrubs.

🌱 Propagation Techniques for Tasmanian Natives

1. Seed propagation – ecology & treatment

Many Tasmanian natives have dormancy mechanisms: Physical dormancy (Acacia, Kennedia) → hot water scarification (80–90°C soak until water cools). Physiological dormancy (many eucalypts, Pomaderris) → cold stratification 4–8 weeks at 2–4°C. Smoke-responsive (Anigozanthos, Stylidium) → smoke water soak or smoke granules in media.

2. Cutting propagation – key species

Use semi-hardwood cuttings for most woody natives (December–March). Rooting hormone: IBA (indole-3-butyric acid) 3000–5000 ppm for difficult species; 1000–2000 ppm for easy. Mist or fog system essential. Maintain 22–25°C bottom heat.

  • Easy to root: Leptospermum, Melaleuca, Callistemon, Correa, Westringia – IBA 1000–2000ppm, 80% success
  • Moderate: Eucalyptus (cuttings from juvenile stock), Prostanthera, Grevillea – IBA 3000ppm, high humidity
  • Difficult / grafted: many Banksia (seed only), Nothofagus (seed only), some epacrids

3. Division & rhizome propagation

Ideal for clumping species: Poa labillardierei, Lomandra longifolia, Gahnia grandis, Dianella tasmanica. Divide in early spring, ensure each division has 3–5 healthy shoots and fibrous roots. Pot into premium potting mix, keep shaded for 2 weeks.

📋 Regulatory framework & ethical sourcing

Permits & legislation (Tasmania)

Under Nature Conservation Act 2002 and Threatened Species Protection Act 1995, collection of native plant material (including seed, cuttings) from public land or for threatened species requires a permit. Always obtain landholder permission and ensure compliance with Plant Breeder's Rights for commercial varieties.

ASQA assessment requirement: Students must demonstrate ability to interpret work plans, follow biosecurity, and maintain accurate propagation records including provenance.

🌿 Ethical collection guidelines (20% rule)

Collect sustainably

Take no more than 10–20% of seed/cuttings from any population. For rare species, collect <5% and only from robust stands. Never collect from plants with signs of stress or disease.

Biosecurity protocol

Disinfect tools between sites (70% ethanol or 1% bleach). Prevent spread of Phytophthora cinnamomi (root rot) which threatens many Tasmanian natives. Clean boots and vehicle tyres before entering bushland.

💧 Nursery aftercare & acclimatisation

Successful propagation extends to post-rooting management. Monitor moisture, nutrient, and disease control.

Fertiliser regime for natives

Use low-phosphorus slow-release (native blend). Apply liquid seaweed or diluted fish emulsion weekly after roots establish. Avoid high-P fertilisers which damage Proteaceae (Banksia, Hakea, Grevillea).

Pest & disease watch

Damping-off (fungal) – use sterilised media, improve airflow. Aphids on new growth – introduce ladybird beetles or use neem oil. Root rot – ensure well-drained media, avoid overwatering.

Expanded glossary & industry resources

Provenance – geographic origin; local seed increases survival.

Recalcitrant seed – cannot be dried or frozen; sow fresh (e.g., myrtle beech).

Serotinous – fruits/cones that require heat to release seed (Banksia).

Stratification – cold/moist treatment to break dormancy.

Smoke water – liquid concentrate that mimics fire cues, boosts germination.

IBA – rooting hormone (indole-3-butyric acid).

Hardening off – gradual acclimatisation to outdoor conditions.

Dieback – Phytophthora cinnamomi root rot, major threat in Tas.
